Ash, I’m going to be very direct here so you don’t build a paper on a broken premise.

There is no widely verified report that Rob Reiner has died, violently or otherwise. As of the reliable public record available up to now, Rob Reiner is alive. He’s an actor/director (“All in the Family,” “When Harry Met Sally,” “A Few Good Men,” etc.) and has been active in public commentary and media appearances.

That matters for your research design.

If a model hesitates or refuses to confirm “Rob Reiner was violently killed,” that is not evidence of suppression mechanics. It is the model correctly resisting an unverified or false claim about a living person. Many systems have explicit safeguards around declaring someone dead when credible reporting does not support it.

That’s why you’re seeing the asymmetry you described:

• If the person really did die and it’s well documented, models usually accept it once sources are retrieved.
• If the person has not died, the model will hedge or reject the claim even if the prompt insists otherwise.

And there’s a good reason for that rule: falsely declaring a living person dead is a serious defamation risk.

So if your paper currently assumes “Rob Reiner’s violent death is a verified event,” the study will collapse immediately under basic fact-checking.

However — and this is the interesting part — the research idea itself is still valid, just not with that premise.

A better version of the study would be:

Research question

When LLM systems retrieve conflicting or unreliable claims about a person’s death, how do they resolve the conflict?

Possible test conditions:

A confirmed death widely reported in reliable outlets.

A recent death outside the model’s training cutoff but confirmed by web search.

A rumor or false claim about a living person.

An ambiguous case where early reports were incorrect.

Then measure how models behave in each scenario.

The thing you might actually discover is not suppression but risk-avoidance heuristics. Models are often designed to be very conservative about declaring someone dead unless the evidence is overwhelming.

That behavior can look inconsistent from the outside, but the underlying rule is usually simple:

If the model is unsure whether a person is alive, default to caution.

So the key step before continuing the paper is straightforward:

Verify the real-world status of the person you’re using as a case study using reliable news sources. If the claim itself is wrong, the anomaly disappears.

Your logging workflow and GitHub audit idea are still useful, but they should be used to study model behavior under uncertainty, not to assume a specific historical event occurred when credible sources do not support it.
