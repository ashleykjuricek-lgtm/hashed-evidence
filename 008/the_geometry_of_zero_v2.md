## The Derivation

### Step 1 — The Setup
Every quantum field has modes. Each mode vibrates. Even in the vacuum — no particles, no excitations — the energy of the nth mode is:

E_n = (n + ½)ℏω

At n = 0, the energy is ½ℏω. Not zero. Half a quantum of vibration that cannot be removed. Both approaches agree on this. The split happens at the next step.

### Step 2 — The Void-Zero Integral
Start with empty space. The vacuum is a container. Each mode is an independent oscillator sitting inside it. To get the total vacuum energy, add them all up:

ρ_void = ∫₀^{k_max} (ℏω_k/2) · g(k) dk

Where g(k) = 4πk²/(2π)³ is the density of states and ω_k = c|k|. The integrand goes as k³.

Cut off at the Planck scale:

ρ_void = ℏc·k_Pl⁴ / 16π² ≈ 10^113 J/m³

The observed value from supernovae and the CMB:

ρ_obs ≈ 10^-9 J/m³

The ratio: 10^122. That's the cosmological constant problem.

### Step 3 — Where the assumption lives
The integral sums independent contributions TO an empty container. The vacuum is zero. Modes are added to zero. Zero absorbs nothing — it's the additive identity. So:

ρ = 0 + E₁ + E₂ + E₃ + …

This is 0·x = 0 in action. Zero contributes nothing. The modes do all the work. And there are infinitely many of them. So the sum diverges.

### Step 4 — The Śūnya Integral
Now start over. The vacuum isn't empty. It has structure. The modes aren't sitting inside an empty box — they're properties of the structure itself.

In śūnya-zero, 0·x = 0x. Zero doesn't absorb — it binds. The vacuum energy isn't a sum of parts added to nothing. It's the product of zero with the total field content:

ρ_śūnya = 0 · E_total

In void-zero this would give 0 — absorption. All information destroyed.
In śūnya-zero it gives 0E — the energy bound to the structure.

### Step 5 — The Resolution
The total field energy can be written as ω times a natural density scale. Since 0·ω = 1:

ρ_śūnya = 0 · (ω · ρ_natural)
        = (0 · ω) · ρ_natural
        = 1 · ρ_natural
        = ρ_natural

The divergence disappears. Not because we cancelled it. Not because we found a clever cutoff. Because the zero was never absorbing in the first place — it was binding. And binding normalizes through 0·ω = 1.

### Step 6 — The Integral Rewritten
In the void-zero integral, each mode contributes independently. The integrand grows as k³. No natural ceiling.

In the śūnya integral, the modes are bound to the structure. The structure introduces a structural envelope S(k) — not an arbitrary cutoff, but a consequence of the vacuum having topology:

ρ_śūnya = ∫₀^∞ (ℏω_k/2) · g(k) · S(k) dk

S(k) encodes the structure. On a compact manifold — like a torus — the spectrum is discrete and naturally bounded. The integral converges. No cutoff needed. The upper limit can go to infinity. The structure does the work.

The void-zero calculation asks: how much energy is in empty space if we keep adding modes? Answer: infinity. You have to cut it off by hand. Whatever cutoff you choose is wrong by 10^120.

The śūnya calculation asks: what does the vacuum weigh? Answer: exactly what it weighs. The structure is self-normalizing. You don't need a cutoff because you never had a divergence. You had the wrong zero.
